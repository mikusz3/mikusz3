RetroTVRaspberryPi

This project uses the following components:

  • Raspberry Pi 4B 1GB
  • PC Remote
  • Server PC running Jellyfin and ErsatzTV

    • Have you ever wondered how to bring Jetix back on air, but without begging Disney for it? I did it! RetroTVRaspberryPi is a project that allows me to watch old TV channels that were once available in Poland when I was a kid.

    I made it work — but it required a lot of thinking and figuring out how to use Jellyfin and ErsatzTV.

    Just like I did, you can do it too. I’ll explain it in simple terms how I did it. Just please don’t ask me obvious questions — this tutorial is for those who are already somewhat familiar, not for people who won’t lift a finger.

    Let’s get started!

    Getting Started

    To begin with, you’ll need to gather recordings of old shows and cartoons, especially those childhood favorites.

    For legal reasons, I can't tell you where to find these recordings, but I suggest checking your drawers for old VHS tapes or DVDs.

    Once you have the recordings, you’ll want to add them to Jellyfin. That part is pretty easy — and I’ll link some guides on how to set up Jellyfin if needed.

    Next, you’ll need to dive into ErsatzTV, which handles the creation of custom TV channels.

    When it comes to ErsatzTV, I highly recommend watching chudy dredd’s video about “Dexter’s Hood Lab,” as well as his “Watching Old TV Channels (Jetix in 2024)” — both are full of useful info about ErsatzTV.

    ErsatzTV

    ErsatzTV is software that lets you create your own TV channels using recordings stored in Jellyfin. With it, you can make a channel that plays your favorite shows or cartoons in either random or planned order.

    Think of it as FAST channels before FAST was a thing — but with more thinking involved about fillers, timing, and whatnot.

    Creating a Channel

    Start by creating a channel: assign it a number, upload a logo that shows up in the schedule and on-screen overlay.

    Next, add your shows. If you've linked Jellyfin to ErsatzTV, it will automatically pull your media library.

    Managing Fillers

    • Pre-Roll – I use this for station idents.
    • Mid-Roll – for breaks between episodes, though I don’t really use it.
    • Post-Roll – what plays after a program ends.
    • Tail – something to play before the next cartoon, e.g. ads or promos.
    • Fallback – a looping filler to fill in remaining gaps.

    Schedules

    In the Schedules section, you organize when each program airs. For me, I used scheduled shows with Post-Roll fillers ("Coming Up Next...") and Tail fillers (ads).

    Playout

    Playout is made by assigning a schedule to it, and optionally setting a fallback schedule too.

    The Final Result

    Once everything is working and your channels are created, you can enjoy retro TV by plugging it into Kodi and adding your ErsatzTV stream URLs.

    The downside? You’ll need to keep your server PC on 24/7 — so you’ll need a compromise if you want your TV on your Pi instead.

    In my case, I use a Raspberry Pi 4B 1GB connected to an old NORDMENDE Galaxy 36 TV via RF. To get it working, I just needed an RF antenna cable and an AV-to-RF converter.
